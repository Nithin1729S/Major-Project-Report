\chapter{RESULTS AND ANALYSIS}

This chapter presents the experimental results and analysis for the two problem 
domains addressed in this work. The first part evaluates the proposed role-aware 
log anomaly detection framework on standard benchmark datasets. The second part 
presents the work done and experimental evaluation of the proposed learning-based 
reconstruction framework for secure image sharing using XOR-based visual 
cryptography.

\section{Problem 1: Log Anomaly Detection}
This section presents the experimental results to verify the proposed framework of role-aware log anomaly detection. We have conducted all experiments on four publicly available benchmark datasets, commonly used in log analysis research. The proposed framework is compared to traditional machine learning algorithms as well as deep learning algorithms. NeuralLog is used as a primary reference point because our framework is a natural extension of this algorithm.

The experimental design is similar to the NeuralLog \cite{neurallog2021} algorithm. We have used a random split on block ID with a ratio of 80/20 on HDFS. Temporal splitting with a ratio of 80/20 is used on BGL, Thunderbird, and Spirit datasets. The fixed-size window approach with a window size of 20 and a step size of 1 is used on all datasets.

The metrics used to measure the performance of our framework are Precision, Recall, and F1-Score.

The baseline algorithms used in this experiment are traditional machine learning algorithms such as Logistic Regression (LR) \cite{bodik2010fingerprinting}, Support Vector Machine (SVM)~\cite{chen2004failure}, and Invariant Mining (IM)~\cite{lou2010mining}, as well as deep learning algorithms such as LogRobust~\cite{zhang2019robust}, Log2Vec~\cite{meng2020log2vec}, and NeuralLog~\cite{neurallog2021}.

\subsection*{Observations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Consistent improvement over NeuralLog:} We outperform NeuralLog on all four datasets. The improvement in F1-score over NeuralLog is +0.01 on HDFS, +0.01 on BGL, +0.02 on Thunderbird, and +0.01 on Spirit. The largest improvement is on Thunderbird, a dataset with high semantic ambiguity and class imbalance, where the effectiveness of role-aware representations is particularly high.
    \item \textbf{Perfect Recall on HDFS and Thunderbird:} We achieve perfect recall on both HDFS and Thunderbird datasets, i.e., 1.00. This is particularly important in production scenarios, where failure to capture anomalies can have serious consequences.
    \item \textbf{Failure of Traditional Methods on BGL:} The F1-scores reported by LR, SVM, and IM on BGL are as low as 0.18 and at most 0.46. This is because parsing error rates on this dataset are high. We avoid these pitfalls because our model and NeuralLog operate on raw text.
    \item \textbf{Strong Result on Spirit:} On Spirit, our model achieves an F1-score of 0.98, slightly higher than NeuralLog's 0.97. The precision is 0.99 and recall is 0.98.
\end{itemize}

    more on textual patterns for anomaly identification, so numeric tokens contribute 
    less, though their retention still provides a marginal improvement.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Impact of Number of Role Categories}

We evaluated F1-scores using 3, 5, 7, and 10 role categories across all datasets 
to determine the optimal number of roles. The results confirm that performance 
peaks at 5 roles, which best balances representational expressiveness against the 
risk of overfitting. Increasing to 7 or 10 roles does not yield further improvement 
and slightly degrades performance on smaller datasets, suggesting that additional 
roles begin to fragment meaningful token clusters unnecessarily.

    \item \textbf{Alignment with semantic expectations:} Despite receiving no

\subsection{Robustness to Out-of-Vocabulary Tokens}

Following the OOV analysis protocol of NeuralLog, we evaluate robustness under a 
60/40 temporal split on BGL, where the test set contains a high proportion of 
templates unseen during training. Under this configuration, the OOV rate in the 
test set reaches 94.12\%. Our model maintains an F1-score of 0.99 under this 
condition, demonstrating that WordPiece subword tokenization combined with 
role-aware representations generalizes effectively to unseen log formats, with no 
degradation relative to the standard evaluation split.

\section{Problem 2: Attack on XOR-Based Secure Image Sharing using Autoencoders}

This section presents the experimental evaluation of learning-based reconstruction
attacks on a $(4,4)$ XOR-based visual secret sharing scheme. All experiments use
plain random XOR share generation.

Two decoder configurations are evaluated:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Configuration 1:} Decoder trained using all four XOR shares.
    \item \textbf{Configuration 2:} Decoder trained using three consecutive XOR shares.
\end{itemize}

Reconstruction quality is evaluated using Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM)
and Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR).

%--------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Configuration 1: ML-Based Decoding Trained on All Four XOR Shares}
In this configuration, the decoder is trained using all four XOR-generated shares
concatenated along the channel dimension. The model learns to approximate the XOR
reconstruction function without explicitly performing XOR operations during inference.

\subsubsection{Evaluation under Missing Share Conditions}
To simulate constrained access scenarios, the trained model was further evaluated
under incomplete share availability. Missing shares were replaced with zero-valued
(blank) channels during inference.
The following cases were analyzed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item One share missing,
    \item Two shares missing,
    \item Three shares missing (only one share available).
\end{itemize}
As expected, reconstruction quality degrades significantly as the number of
available shares decreases. When only three shares are available, partial structural
information may be inferred due to dataset priors; however, visual fidelity is
substantially reduced. With only one or two shares, the decoder fails to recover
meaningful content.
These results demonstrate that although the model learns an effective reconstruction
mapping under full-share training, the absence of required shares significantly
impairs reconstruction performance.

\subsubsection{Experimental Results: Reconstruction Failure under Missing Shares}

To empirically validate the degradation described above, the trained decoder was
tested under two explicit missing-share scenarios: one share missing and two shares
missing. In both cases, the absent shares were substituted with zero-valued channels,
and the model was queried for reconstruction without any retraining or fine-tuning.

The visual outcomes of these experiments are presented in
Figures~\ref{fig:xor_1missing} and~\ref{fig:xor_2missing}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/xor_missing1.png}
\caption{Reconstruction attempt with one XOR share missing. The decoder fails to
recover meaningful image content, producing severely distorted or unintelligible
outputs.}
\label{fig:xor_1missing}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/xor_missing2.png}
\caption{Reconstruction attempt with two XOR shares missing. With only half of the
required shares available, reconstruction is not possible; the model output bears
no resemblance to the original image.}
\label{fig:xor_2missing}
\end{figure}

In both scenarios, \textbf{reconstruction was not possible}. The outputs produced
by the decoder under these conditions were visually incoherent, with no discernible
structural or semantic resemblance to the original images. This outcome is consistent
with the theoretical properties of XOR-based secret sharing: since the scheme
requires all shares to recover the secret, the absence of even a single share
renders the information-theoretically irretrievable without additional assumptions
or priors.

These findings confirm that the ML-based decoder, despite its strong performance
under full-share availability, does not circumvent the fundamental security
guarantees of the underlying XOR scheme. The model cannot hallucinate or infer
missing share content from the remaining inputs alone, thereby preserving the
integrity of the secret sharing framework.

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

The experimental results establish a clear hierarchy:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Near-lossless reconstruction is achieved when all four XOR shares are available (configuration 1).
    \item Reconstruction quality degrades significantly under missing-share conditions, confirming the structural dependency of the XOR scheme.
    \item A decoder trained solely on three consecutive shares (configuration 2) fails to recover meaningful image content, reinforcing the security guarantees of the $(4,4)$ XOR construction.
\end{itemize}

Overall, while neural networks can learn the XOR reconstruction mapping under full share availability, they do not invalidate the theoretical security of the scheme when fewer than the required number of shares are accessible.
